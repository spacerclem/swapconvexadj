\section{Assessment of the OIS payment-lag convexity assumption}
\label{sec:ois_payment_lag_convexity}

\subsection{Scope and assumption under review}

The OIS pricing implementation under review assumes that no convexity adjustment is required for the floating leg, although each floating coupon is paid with a payment lag of two business days after the last fixing date of the compounded overnight rate. In other words, for a coupon accruing over $[S_i,E_i]$ and paid at $P_i:=E_i+\ell$ with $\ell \approx 2$ business days, the implementation prices the coupon as if the payoff were effectively aligned with $E_i$.

This simplifying assumption is standard in production curve-building environments when the payment lag is short and the expected impact is immaterial. Nevertheless, from a rigorous risk-neutral valuation perspective, the assumption is not exact and gives rise to a convexity term.

\subsection{Why a convexity term arises}

Consider one floating coupon of notional $N$ accruing over $[S_i,E_i]$, paid at $P_i>E_i$. In continuous-compounding notation, define
\[
Y_i:=\int_{S_i}^{E_i} r_u \, du,
\qquad
\Pi_i := N\left(e^{Y_i}-1\right).
\]
The exact time-$t$ present value ($t\le S_i$) is
\[
PV_i(t)=N\,\mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[D(t,P_i)\left(e^{Y_i}-1\right)\right],
\]
where
\[
D(t,T):=\exp\!\left(-\int_t^T r_u\,du\right).
\]

If the payment date were aligned with the end of compounding, i.e.\ $P_i=E_i$, one would have the standard identity
\[
\mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[D(t,E_i)e^{Y_i}\right]=P(t,S_i),
\]
hence
\[
PV_i^{(\ell=0)}(t)=N\bigl(P(t,S_i)-P(t,E_i)\bigr).
\]

When $P_i>E_i$, the exact value becomes
\[
PV_i(t)=N\,\mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[D(t,P_i)e^{Y_i}\right]-N P(t,P_i).
\]
Writing the first expectation as a product plus covariance,
\[
\mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[D(t,P_i)e^{Y_i}\right]
=
\mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[D(t,P_i)\right]
\mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left[e^{Y_i}\right]
+
\operatorname{Cov}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\!\left(D(t,P_i),e^{Y_i}\right),
\]
shows directly that a non-zero covariance term is present whenever the random payoff, known only at $E_i$, is discounted to a later date $P_i$.

Equivalently, using the $P_i$-forward measure,
\[
PV_i(t)=N P(t,P_i)\left(\mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}^{P_i}}\!\left[e^{Y_i}\right]-1\right).
\]
The quantity $e^{Y_i}$ is naturally associated with the end-of-accrual date $E_i$, whereas discounting is performed with numeraire $P(\cdot,P_i)$. Since $E_i\neq P_i$, the measure change between the ``natural'' end-of-fixing valuation point and the payment-date numeraire induces the convexity term. The pricing shortcut consists precisely in neglecting this mismatch.

\subsection{Toy-model quantification under a Gaussian short-rate diffusion}

To quantify the order of magnitude of the omission, consider a one-factor Gaussian short-rate model with zero mean reversion:
\[
dr_t = \sigma(t)\, dW_t^{\mathbb{Q}},
\]
with deterministic volatility function $\sigma(\cdot)$. This is the $a=0$ limit of Hull--White and is sufficient for a first-order materiality estimate.

For a short payment lag $\ell:=P_i-E_i$, define
\[
X_i := \int_{E_i}^{P_i} r_u\,du.
\]
Since $\ell$ is small, a first-order approximation gives
\[
X_i \approx \ell\, r_{E_i}.
\]
Under the Gaussian model, $r_{E_i}$ is normally distributed. Denote by $\nu(E_i)$ the normal implied volatility of the relevant short-rate caplet with expiry $E_i$; then, to first order,
\[
\operatorname{Var}(r_{E_i}) \approx \nu(E_i)^2 E_i,
\]
hence
\[
\operatorname{Var}(X_i)\approx \ell^2 \nu(E_i)^2 E_i.
\]

Because $X_i$ is Gaussian,
\[
\mathbb{E}\!\left[e^{-X_i}\right]
=
\exp\!\left(-\mathbb{E}[X_i]+\frac{1}{2}\operatorname{Var}(X_i)\right).
\]
The deterministic carry effect is captured by the term $-\mathbb{E}[X_i]$; the \emph{pure convexity correction} is therefore driven by
\[
CA_i
\approx
\exp\!\left(\frac{1}{2}\operatorname{Var}(X_i)\right)-1
\approx
\frac{1}{2}\ell^2 \nu(E_i)^2 E_i.
\]
Accordingly, the omitted convexity effect is second order in the payment lag and quadratic in normal volatility.

\subsection{Approximate impact on the par rate of an OIS swap}

Let $K(T)$ denote the par fixed rate of an OIS swap of maturity $T$, and let
\[
A(T):=\sum_{i=1}^{n(T)} \alpha_i P(t,P_i)
\]
denote the fixed-leg annuity. If the floating-leg present value is shifted by $\Delta PV_{\mathrm{flt}}(T)$, the induced shift in the par rate is
\[
\Delta K(T) \approx \frac{\Delta PV_{\mathrm{flt}}(T)}{N\,A(T)}.
\]

Under a flat-term approximation, replacing coupon-specific expiries by the swap maturity and using $\nu(E_i)\approx \nu(T)$, one obtains the practical closed-form estimate
\[
\boxed{
\Delta K(T)\approx \frac{1}{2}\,\ell\,T\,\nu(T)^2
}
\]
with:
\begin{itemize}
\item $T$ expressed in years,
\item $\ell$ expressed in years (for a two-business-day lag, $\ell \approx 2/365$ under ACT/365 or $\ell \approx 2/360$ under ACT/360),
\item $\nu(T)$ the normal implied volatility in absolute rate units (e.g.\ $50$bp $\mapsto 0.0050$).
\end{itemize}

The corresponding impact in basis points is
\[
\Delta K_{\mathrm{bp}}(T)
=
10^4 \times \Delta K(T)
=
10^4 \times \frac{1}{2}\,\ell\,T\,\nu(T)^2.
\]

This formula is intentionally simple and conservative. It is not intended as a production repricing formula, but as a validation-side materiality proxy.

\subsection{Approximate impact on portfolio value using EOD IR deltas}

Let $\Delta^{1\mathrm{bp}}(T)$ denote the portfolio sensitivity, obtained from end-of-day risk batches, to a 1bp bump of the JPY OIS par rate at tenor $T$:
\[
\Delta^{1\mathrm{bp}}(T)
:=
\frac{\partial V}{\partial K_{\mathrm{bp}}(T)}.
\]
Then the first-order portfolio value impact of the omitted convexity at tenor $T$ is approximated by
\[
\boxed{
\Delta V(T)\approx \Delta^{1\mathrm{bp}}(T)\,\Delta K_{\mathrm{bp}}(T)
}
\]
and the total portfolio impact is
\[
\boxed{
\Delta V_{\mathrm{tot}}\approx \sum_T \Delta^{1\mathrm{bp}}(T)\,\Delta K_{\mathrm{bp}}(T).
}
\]

When a conservative materiality view is desired, one may also monitor the absolute aggregation
\[
\Delta V_{\mathrm{tot}}^{\mathrm{abs}}:=\sum_T \left|\Delta^{1\mathrm{bp}}(T)\,\Delta K_{\mathrm{bp}}(T)\right|.
\]

\subsection{Validation interpretation}

The assumption may be considered acceptable when both of the following conditions hold:
\begin{enumerate}
\item the tenor-level induced par-rate shifts remain well below typical market bid--ask spreads for OIS swaps; as a practical benchmark, shifts below $1$bp are not considered concerning;
\item the resulting portfolio value impact remains immaterial relative to the desk materiality threshold; in the present validation context, impacts below JPY $1{,}000{,}000$ are not considered concerning.
\end{enumerate}

The numerical results produced from the sensitivity-based approximation described above should therefore be interpreted against these two thresholds.

def tenor_to_years(tenor: str) -> float:
    """
    Convert tenor strings such as '3M', '6M', '1Y', '18M', '10Y' to year fractions.
    """
    tenor = tenor.strip().upper()
    match = re.fullmatch(r"(\d+)\s*([DWMY])", tenor)
    if not match:
        raise ValueError(f"Unsupported tenor format: {tenor}")
    value = int(match.group(1))
    unit = match.group(2)

    if unit == "D":
        return value / 365.0
    elif unit == "W":
        return value * 7.0 / 365.0
    elif unit == "M":
        return value / 12.0
    elif unit == "Y":
        return float(value)
    else:
        raise ValueError(f"Unsupported tenor unit: {unit}")

def prepare_input_frames(
    df_delta: pd.DataFrame,
    df_vol: pd.DataFrame,
    tenor_col_delta: str = None,
    delta_col: str = None,
    tenor_col_vol: str = None,
    vol_col: str = None
) -> pd.DataFrame:
    """
    Standardize and merge the input dataframes.
    """
    if tenor_col_delta is None:
        tenor_col_delta = df_delta.columns[0]
    if delta_col is None:
        delta_col = df_delta.columns[1]

    if tenor_col_vol is None:
        tenor_col_vol = df_vol.columns[0]
    if vol_col is None:
        vol_col = df_vol.columns[1]

    left = df_delta[[tenor_col_delta, delta_col]].copy()
    left.columns = ["tenor", "ir_delta_1bp_jpy"]

    right = df_vol[[tenor_col_vol, vol_col]].copy()
    right.columns = ["tenor", "normal_vol_bp"]

    merged = pd.merge(left, right, on="tenor", how="inner")
    merged["tenor_years"] = merged["tenor"].apply(tenor_to_years)

    return merged

def compute_convexity_impact(
    df_delta: pd.DataFrame,
    df_vol: pd.DataFrame,
    lag_days: float = 2.0,
    day_count_base: float = 365.0,
    tenor_col_delta: str = None,
    delta_col: str = None,
    tenor_col_vol: str = None,
    vol_col: str = None
) -> pd.DataFrame:
    """
    Implements the approximation:
        ΔK(T) ≈ 0.5 * lag * T * ν(T)^2

    where:
        lag = lag_days / day_count_base
        T   = maturity in years
        ν   = normal vol in absolute units (e.g. 25 bp -> 0.0025)

    Then:
        ΔK_bp(T) = 1e4 * ΔK(T)
        ΔV(T)    = IRDelta_1bp(T) * ΔK_bp(T)

    Returns a dataframe with tenor-level impacts.
    """
    df = prepare_input_frames(
        df_delta=df_delta,
        df_vol=df_vol,
        tenor_col_delta=tenor_col_delta,
        delta_col=delta_col,
        tenor_col_vol=tenor_col_vol,
        vol_col=vol_col
    ).copy()

    lag_years = lag_days / day_count_base

    # Convert normal vol from bp to absolute rate units
    df["normal_vol_abs"] = df["normal_vol_bp"] / 10000.0

    # Analytical approximation for par-rate shift
    df["par_rate_shift_abs"] = 0.5 * lag_years * df["tenor_years"] * (df["normal_vol_abs"] ** 2)
    df["par_rate_shift_bp"] = 10000.0 * df["par_rate_shift_abs"]

    # Approximate value impact in JPY
    df["portfolio_impact_jpy"] = df["ir_delta_1bp_jpy"] * df["par_rate_shift_bp"]
    df["abs_portfolio_impact_jpy"] = df["portfolio_impact_jpy"].abs()

    # Reorder / presentation columns
    out = df[[
        "tenor",
        "tenor_years",
        "normal_vol_bp",
        "par_rate_shift_bp",
        "ir_delta_1bp_jpy",
        "portfolio_impact_jpy",
        "abs_portfolio_impact_jpy"
    ]].copy()

    return out

def make_summary_row(df_result: pd.DataFrame) -> pd.DataFrame:
    """
    Add a total row.
    """
    total_signed = df_result["portfolio_impact_jpy"].sum()
    total_abs = df_result["abs_portfolio_impact_jpy"].sum()
    max_rate_shift_bp = df_result["par_rate_shift_bp"].abs().max()

    summary = pd.DataFrame([{
        "tenor": "Total",
        "tenor_years": np.nan,
        "normal_vol_bp": np.nan,
        "par_rate_shift_bp": max_rate_shift_bp,  # keep max rate shift for quick reading
        "ir_delta_1bp_jpy": np.nan,
        "portfolio_impact_jpy": total_signed,
        "abs_portfolio_impact_jpy": total_abs
    }])

    return pd.concat([df_result, summary], ignore_index=True)

def dataframe_to_latex(df_result: pd.DataFrame, caption: str = None, label: str = None) -> str:
    """
    Export the results table to LaTeX.
    """
    fmt_df = df_result.copy()

    # Formatting
    fmt_df["tenor_years"] = fmt_df["tenor_years"].map(lambda x: "" if pd.isna(x) else f"{x:.4f}")
    fmt_df["normal_vol_bp"] = fmt_df["normal_vol_bp"].map(lambda x: "" if pd.isna(x) else f"{x:.2f}")
    fmt_df["par_rate_shift_bp"] = fmt_df["par_rate_shift_bp"].map(lambda x: f"{x:.6f}")
    fmt_df["ir_delta_1bp_jpy"] = fmt_df["ir_delta_1bp_jpy"].map(lambda x: "" if pd.isna(x) else f"{x:,.0f}")
    fmt_df["portfolio_impact_jpy"] = fmt_df["portfolio_impact_jpy"].map(lambda x: f"{x:,.0f}")
    fmt_df["abs_portfolio_impact_jpy"] = fmt_df["abs_portfolio_impact_jpy"].map(lambda x: f"{x:,.0f}")

    latex = fmt_df.to_latex(
        index=False,
        escape=False,
        caption=caption,
        label=label
    )
    return latex

\section{Purpose of the Analysis}

This section evaluates the materiality of a simplifying assumption used in the OIS swap pricing framework.  

The production implementation assumes that no convexity adjustment is required when pricing the floating leg of an OIS swap, despite the fact that floating coupons are paid two business days after the final overnight fixing date.

While this approximation is common market practice due to the small magnitude of the payment lag, a rigorous risk-neutral valuation reveals the presence of a convexity term.  

The objective of this analysis is therefore:

\begin{enumerate}
\item to explain why a convexity term arises under rigorous risk-neutral pricing,
\item to derive a simple analytical proxy for the magnitude of the effect using a Gaussian short-rate model,
\item to approximate the impact of the assumption on the valuation of the OIS portfolio using end-of-day sensitivity measures.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Origin of the Convexity Term}

Consider a floating coupon accruing between dates $S_i$ and $E_i$, paid at $P_i = E_i + \ell$ where $\ell$ is the payment lag.

Define

\[
Y_i = \int_{S_i}^{E_i} r_u du
\]

The floating coupon payoff is

\[
\Pi_i = N \left(e^{Y_i}-1\right)
\]

The exact present value is therefore

\[
PV_i(t)=
N\mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}
\left[
D(t,P_i)(e^{Y_i}-1)
\right]
\]

where

\[
D(t,T)=\exp\left(-\int_t^T r_u du\right)
\]

If the payment date coincided with the end of the compounding period ($P_i=E_i$), the standard identity holds

\[
\mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[D(t,E_i)e^{Y_i}\right]=P(t,S_i)
\]

leading to the familiar result

\[
PV_i=N(P(t,S_i)-P(t,E_i))
\]

However when $P_i>E_i$,

\[
PV_i=N\mathbb{E}_t^{\mathbb{Q}}\left[D(t,P_i)e^{Y_i}\right]-NP(t,P_i)
\]

Expanding the expectation yields

\[
\mathbb{E}_t[D(t,P_i)e^{Y_i}]
=
\mathbb{E}_t[D(t,P_i)]
\mathbb{E}_t[e^{Y_i}]
+
Cov_t(D(t,P_i),e^{Y_i})
\]

The covariance term appears because the floating payoff is determined at $E_i$ while discounting occurs at $P_i$.

Equivalently using the $P_i$-forward measure,

\[
PV_i=N P(t,P_i)\left(\mathbb{E}_t^{Q^{P_i}}[e^{Y_i}]-1\right)
\]

Since $e^{Y_i}$ is naturally associated with the end-of-fixing date $E_i$, the measure change between $E_i$ and $P_i$ introduces a convexity adjustment.

The production assumption corresponds to neglecting this covariance term.

\section{Toy Model for Quantification}

To estimate the magnitude of the convexity effect we consider a Gaussian short-rate diffusion

\[
dr_t = \sigma(t)dW_t
\]

with zero mean reversion.

Define

\[
X_i = \int_{E_i}^{P_i} r_u du
\]

For small $\ell$ we approximate

\[
X_i \approx \ell r_{E_i}
\]

Assuming a normal implied volatility $\nu(E_i)$ for the short rate

\[
Var(r_{E_i}) \approx \nu(E_i)^2 E_i
\]

which implies

\[
Var(X_i) \approx \ell^2 \nu(E_i)^2 E_i
\]

Since $X_i$ is Gaussian,

\[
\mathbb{E}[e^{-X_i}]
=
\exp\left(-\mathbb{E}[X_i]+\frac12 Var(X_i)\right)
\]

The convexity component is therefore

\[
CA_i \approx
\exp\left(\frac12 Var(X_i)\right)-1
\]

Using the variance approximation,

\[
CA_i \approx \frac12 \ell^2 \nu(E_i)^2 E_i
\]

The convexity adjustment is therefore second order in the payment lag and quadratic in volatility.

\section{Impact on the Par Swap Rate}

Let the swap annuity be

\[
A(T)=\sum_i \alpha_i P(t,P_i)
\]

A floating leg valuation error $\Delta PV$ induces a shift in the par rate

\[
\Delta K(T) \approx \frac{\Delta PV}{NA(T)}
\]

Using the Gaussian approximation and replacing coupon expiries by the swap maturity $T$ gives

\[
\boxed{
\Delta K(T) \approx
\frac12 \ell T \nu(T)^2
}
\]

The par-rate impact in basis points becomes

\[
\Delta K_{bp}(T)=10^4\frac12\ell T\nu(T)^2
\]

where

\begin{itemize}
\item $\ell$ is the payment lag in years
\item $T$ is the swap maturity
\item $\nu(T)$ is the normal implied volatility
\end{itemize}

\section{Portfolio Value Impact}

Let

\[
\Delta^{1bp}(T)
\]

denote the portfolio sensitivity to a 1bp shift in the OIS par rate at tenor $T$.

The approximate portfolio impact is

\[
\Delta V(T)=\Delta^{1bp}(T)\Delta K_{bp}(T)
\]

The total portfolio effect is therefore

\[
\Delta V_{total}=\sum_T \Delta^{1bp}(T)\Delta K_{bp}(T)
\]

\section{Materiality Criteria}

The assumption is considered acceptable if

\begin{itemize}
\item Par-rate shifts remain below typical OIS bid-ask spreads (approximately 1bp)
\item Total portfolio impact remains below JPY 1,000,000
\end{itemize}
